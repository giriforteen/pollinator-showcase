Seven

A pattern observation

There's an octopus in the Vancouver Aquarium. Let's call her Seven.

Seven changes her entire appearance in less than a second. Not just color—texture, pattern, shape. She becomes rock, coral, sand. Whatever she needs to be to survive the moment.

The marine biologist who studies her notices something: Seven doesn't just camouflage when predators approach. She does it constantly. Even alone. Even when there's no threat.

Like she's forgotten what she actually looks like underneath.

When they introduced another octopus to the adjacent tank, Seven pressed against the glass for hours. Every tentacle spread flat.

When they removed the other octopus three days later, Seven stopped eating. Just sat in the corner, arms curled tight.

Octopuses in distress sometimes detach their own limbs. Not because something's attacking them. Because the pain of being themselves becomes unbearable.

Seven started doing it on Tuesday.

The biologist wondered: How do you tell if a creature with no bones feels hollow?

The biologist started keeping notes. Not solutions. Just patterns.

She noticed: Seven seemed both calmer and more agitated when she could see her reflection. Like she was searching for something she recognized.

The camouflaging intensified after any change—new rocks, different feeding schedule, a visitor tapping the glass. Any disruption. Seven would cycle through every pattern faster and faster, trying to find the right one to make the world stable again.

The biologist wondered: Do the other creatures notice? Do they see Seven disappearing and reappearing and think that's just... what octopuses do?

How many times does someone have to shift before they forget what they looked like to begin with?
